How the bladder works

Urine is made in the kidneys, then travels down to the bladder in 2 tubes called ureters

The bladder is made of a muscular wall that stretches and fills with urine as it is made

As the muscle of the bladder stretches, signals are sent to the brain that the bladder is filling in order to prepare to empty the bladder

Then, if it's an appropriate time, signals are sent back to the bladder to contract or squeeze the bladder muscle

The urine leaves the bladder through a small ring of muscle called a sphincter which is a gate-keeper: it stays tight to keep urine in, and relaxes to allow urine out

In order to get all the urine out of the bladder

  • The bladder muscle must contract
  • The sphincter must relax

The signals to coordinate these two actions are disrupted in SCI in different ways based on if the injury is Upper Motor Neuron (UMN) or Lower Motor Neuron (LMN)

The goal for management is to stay dry, empty the bladder, and prevent problems like infections or kidney damage

Upper Motor Neuron Features

The bladder is small, tight, and the muscle is overactive, called spastic

Even a small amount of urine causes the bladder muscle to squeeze and push urine out without warning

The signals that the brain sends down to keep the urine inside and allow urine to be stored in the bladder do not reach the bladder

The sphincter (gatekeeper) muscle is also tight and spastic and the signals from the brain to relax it to allow urine out may be disrupted

Therefore as urine is made, the bladder continuously tries to squeeze it out, sometimes reaching resistance from the sphincter which is closed tight

Urine leaks when the pressure in the bladder is more than the strength of the sphincter muscle

  • That can cause a very high pressure environment in the bladder which can be dangerous
  • If the pressure is high enough, some urine can be pushed backwards to the ureters and kidneys, causing kidney damage
How to Manage

Speak with a medical provider about what is best

Some commonly used methods to empty the bladder include:

  • Intermittent Catheterization
  • Suprapubic Catheter
  • Condom Catheter

Sometimes, medications can be given to relax the bladder or to better allow urine to flow out

If medications are not enough, there are injections that can be given into the bladder, or surgeries to help best empty the bladder

Lower Motor Neuron Features

The bladder is large, weak and the muscle is non-functioning, called flaccid

The signals that usually let the brain know that the bladder is full do not reach the brain

Therefore the bladder fills beyond its usual capacity

The sphincter (gatekeeper) muscle is also weak and it is difficult to keep urine stored inside the bladder

Urine can spill out after the bladder fills past a certain point
