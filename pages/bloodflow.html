Blood Pressure changes after SCI

After a spinal cord injury, some people have lower than usual blood pressure because the messages the body usually uses to maintain a normal blood pressure are disrupted. There can be quick drops in blood pressure when sitting or standing up called orthostatic hypotension. "Orthostatic" means based on position, "hypo-" means low, and "-tension" is referring to the blood pressure in the body.

Orthostatic hypotension can cause:

  • Dizziness
  • Light-headedness
  • Blurry vision or seeing starts
  • Confusion
  • Fainting
How to Manage

Speak with a medical provider to discuss orthostatic hypotension as it can cause severe symptoms and fainting

Some common ways to address orthostatic hypotension are:

  • Lie down to immediately help blood pressure go up
  • Abdominal binder (tight binder over the stomach)
  • Compression stockings
  • Using a tilt table to gradually train the body to better maintain blood pressure and adjust to position changes
  • Increasing water and salt intake
  • Medication may be given based on a medical provider's clinical decision
Deep Vein Thrombosis (DVT)

If muscles in the legs are weak or not functioning, risk for blood clot, known as a deep vein thrombosis, forming in the leg can increase in the period immediately after injury.

A DVT is a blood clot that stops the return of blood back up to the heart, so because of the block blood backs up and there is usually swelling in the leg.

Blood clots can be especially dangerous if a part of the clot breaks off and travels up to the lung, causing a pulmonary embolism. This can cause difficulty breathing, and can even block someone's ability to breathe altogether, causing death.

How to Prevent DVT

It is recommended that medication to prevent deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is given for at least 8 weeks after injury This medication can either be injected under the skin or given as a pill by mouth.
