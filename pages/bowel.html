How the bowel works

The colon (large intestine) is the part of the bowel where nutrients are absorbed from digested food to form stool

Stool is squeezed through the colon by muscles in its walls that work by reflex: when the colon wall is stretched by digested food, it squeezes around the food pushing it through

The rectum is the last part of the colon where the stool stays until ready for a bowel movement

The anus, or rectal opening, is made of a sphincter muscle which can be tight to keep stool in, or relax to let stool out in a bowel movement

Upper Motor Neuron Features

Spastic, tight sphincter muscle that keeps the anus closed, holding stool in

A regular bowel routine can help to get the stool out. This can consist of using a reflex to allow release of stool by:

  • Physical stimulation: using a finger to stretch the sphincter muscle and wall of the rectum to cause it to open and push stool out
  • Chemical stimulation with medication inserted in the rectum: suppository, enema, mini-enema
Lower Motor Neuron Features

Flaccid, loose sphincter muscle allows stool through

Usually, stool passes easily from the rectum, so the goal is to only have a bowel movement when ready and not to have accidents

This is done with a regular bowel routine that could consist of:

  • Bulking agent: medication given to bulk up the stool so it is less likely to leak out
  • May have to perform bowel routine more frequently, 2 times a day or at least every day
  • Everyone is different, so speak with your medical provider to create the best plan
