Breathing with SCI

We breathe to bring oxygen into the lungs and transfer it into the blood where it travels to the body to fuel all organs. The organs release their waste product, carbon dioxide, into the blood where it is carried back to the lungs and breathed out

Signals from the brain to the breathing muscles can be affected depending on the level and completeness of injury

C1-C4
  • Muscles Affected:
    • Diaphragm (main breathing muscle)
  • Potential Problems:
    • Unable to take a breath
    • Breaths are too shallow and don't bring in enough oxygen

Individuals with an injury between C1-C4 will sometimes require a ventilator (breathing machine) to assist them in breathing

C5-T11
  • Muscles Affected:
    • Intercostals (muscles between the ribs)
    • Abdominals (below the ribs)
  • Potential Problems:
    • Shallow breaths
    • Difficulty coughing and clearing secretions (fluids that accumulate in the lungs)

Individuals with an injury between C5-C11 may require a ventilator (breathing machine) for a period of time after injury

Because of difficulty coughing, the lungs can become congested

Congested lungs can lead to pneumonia (lung infection)

Shallow breaths can lead to atelectasis, with is a partial collapse of the lung because air isn't reaching that area

How to Address Breathing Problems

Talk to your medical provider for the best ways to address problems that arise for you

Some common ways to deal with breathing problems are:

  • Difficulty with cough
    • Chest percussion: cupped hand taps along the back to loosen secretions in the lung
    • Assisted cough: While coughing, someone assists by presses into the stomach under the ribcage to give more force to the cough
  • Shallow breaths
    • Breathing exercises
    • Training accessory breathing muscles (muscles in the front of the neck and chest) to help in breathing
