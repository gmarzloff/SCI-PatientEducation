Skin Changes after SCI
Pressure Injuries

After a SCI, a person may lose their sense of feeling or pressure on the skin. The skin also becomes more fragile. This can lead to what is called a pressure injury, also known as a pressure ulcer, ulcer, decube, decubitus ulcer.

If a person puts pressure on one body area too long, either sitting or lying down, then that area cannot receive any blood flow, leading to a pressure injury

Pressure injuries can look like redness, a bruise, a cut or a scab. More advanced pressure injuries can become a deep opening in the body, even exposing muscle and bone

Where do pressure Injuries form?

The areas that are most likely to have pressure injuries are where there is the least "padding" between bone and skin

Different bony points are at risk for developing pressure injuries in different position:

  • Lying down on your back
    • Sacrum (lower end of the spine, just below the waist)
    • Tailbone
    • Heels
  • Lying on your side
    • Hip bone
    • Knees
    • Ankles
  • Sitting up
    • Seat bones
How to Prevent Pressure Injuries
  • Frequent repositioning (shifting weight) is necessary to prevent injury
  • Appropriate positioning and cushioning when sitting in a wheelchair, or lying down to bed to sleep
  • Daily skin checks to make sure a pressure injury is not forming
