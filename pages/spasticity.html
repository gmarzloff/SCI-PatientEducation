Spasticity
What is Spasticity?

Spasticity can occur in Upper Motor Neuron spinal cord injuries.

  • Spasticity after SCI is when muscles contract (tighten) or move on their own without signals coming from the brain telling the muscle to move. This happens because of reflexes
  • A reflex is, for example, what is tested by doctors when the knee is tapped and the leg kicks
  • Signals are sent from area tapped (the muscle) to the spinal cord and back, causing the muscle to move
  • No signals are sent to the brain, so reflexes can work even if there is a spinal cord injury
  • All muscles have reflexes all the time, but there are signals sent from the brain to the muscles to keep the reflexes under control
  • After a spinal cord injury, the signals keeping reflexes controlled are blocked so reflexes can be overactive, and muscles can be jumpy or "spastic," moving on their own

Spasticity can be present in arm, torso, and leg muscles, but also in the muscles of internal organs, like the bladder and bowel.

Is spasticity good or bad?

Spasticity just happens to some people after spinal cord injury - it is neither good nor bad!

There are some benefits to spasticity:

  • Spasticity may help maintain muscle size and bone strength because muscles are contracting and moving
  • Spasticity helps circulate blood from the lower body back up to the heart because contracting leg muscles squeeze the veins in the legs carrying blood
  • More spasms can be a sign that something is irritating the body, so increased spasticity can act as a warning to address the issue and prevent a problem
  • Spasticity can be used to tighten a muscle for support when trying to perform a physical task, such as transfer

There are also some drawbacks to spasticity:

  • Spasticity can be uncomfortable or cause cramping or pain
  • It can interfere with activities of daily living like transfers, getting dressed, or driving
  • Spasticity can sometimes cause so much tone in a muscle that it can stop the joint from moving fully. This is called a contracture.
What to do if Spasticity is becoming a problem

Talk to your doctor to discuss whether treating spasticity is right for you

Usually stretching and 'range of motion' exercises are the first recommmendations

If spasticity is still a problem:

  • There are medications that can be taken by mouth
  • Injections can be given directly into the spastic muscles
  • Medication can be given intrathecally - into the spinal fluid - through a pump that is placed by a surgeon under the skin. Doctors will refill the pump regularly depending on the dose of medication needed
  • Surgery can be performed on the nerves to stop the reflex signals to the muscles
